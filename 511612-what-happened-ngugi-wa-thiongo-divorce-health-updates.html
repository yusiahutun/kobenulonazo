    Early life

    Ngũgĩ wa Thiong'o was born on January 5, 1938, in Limuru, Kenya. His father, Thiong'o wa Nducu, was a farmer forced out of his land by British settlers. His mother was Wanjiku wa Ngũgĩ, one of his father’s several wives.

    Ngũgĩ grew up in Kenya at the height of colonial rule when the country was waging war for independence. This colonial conflict heavily affected him, with two of his brothers being killed by British soldiers. His mother was also arrested and tortured for suspicion of offering aid to the freedom fighters.

    Despite the challenges of his childhood, Ngugi managed to build a life for himself through education. He attended Kamaandura (a mission-run school in Limuru) and Karinga School in Mangu for his primary education. In 1955, he was admitted to Alliance High School, becoming one of the few Africans to attend the elite school.

    After his secondary education, Ngũgĩ wa Thiong'o attended Makerere University in Kampala, Uganda. He graduated in 1963 with a Bachelor of Arts degree in English. He then left for England, where he attended Leeds University for his postgraduate studies.

    Career beginnings

    After completing his education, Ngugi started working as a lecturer at the University of Nairobi. During this time, he also held other teaching roles, including working as a creative fellow at Makerere University's Creative Writing program and guest lecturing at Northwestern University in Illinois.

    Ngũgĩ wa Thiong'o has been a teacher for most of his life. While living in exile (after crashing with the Kenyan government), he worked as a visiting English and Comparative Literature professor at Yale University.

    He has also worked as a New York University Literature and Performance Studies professor. He is currently an English and Comparative Literature professor at the University of California, Irvine.

    Writing career

    Ngũgĩ has also enjoyed a prolific career as a writer. He began writing as an undergraduate at Makerere University, where his first work, The Black Hermit (a play), was performed at the National Theatre. His debut novel, Weep Not, Child, was published in 1964 and was one of the first major literary books written by an East African.

    In 1965, Ngũgĩ wa Thiong'o published his second book, The River Between, another literary masterpiece. This was followed by his third book, A Grain of Wheat, in 1967. Ngugi released several more books and plays over the next years, including:

    • Mtaa Mweusi (1970)
    • This Time Tomorrow (play)
    • Secret Lives and Other Stories
    • The Trial of Dedan Kimathi

    Ngũgĩ became disillusioned by the English language, which he saw as a continuation of colonialism. As a result, he renounced Christianity and his baptism name. He also renounced English and started writing in his ethnic Gikuyu language.

    Ngũgĩ wa Thiong'o's wife

    The celebrated Kenyan author married in 1961 while still in college. Unfortunately, not much is known about Ngũgĩ wa Thiong'o's first wife, a woman named Wanjiru. However, they reportedly had six children together.

    When Ngũgĩ was forced into exile, he settled in the U.S.A., where he met his second wife, Njeeri wa Ndung'o. They met in 1989 in New York at the Grand Central Station. They tied the knot in 1992.

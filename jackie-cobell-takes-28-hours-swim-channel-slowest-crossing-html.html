    Woman takes 28 hours to swim Channel in slowest ever crossing... after tides drag her 40 MILES off course

    By Paul Harris for the Daily Mail
    Updated: 05:27 EST, 28 July 2010

    • e-mail

    71

    View
    comments


    The sun was shining, the sea was calm. It must have seemed like the perfect day for a quick swim. Down on the beach, would-be Channel swimmer Jackie Cobell turned her back on the white cliffs of Dover, adjusted her goggles, and boldly set off for Calais. The time was 6.40am.

    A staggering 28 hours and 44 minutes later – after most of France had rested overnight before contemplating lunch the next day – the exhausted, triumphant figure of a 56-year-old mother of two from Kent crawled ashore and walked proudly into the record books.

    After five years in training, Mrs Cobell had just been crowned the slowest person to cross the Channel under her own steam.

    Jackie Cobell spent more than 28 hours in the English Channel after the turning tides continually pulled off course... but eventually made it to Calais

    In a remarkable marathon of grit and determination, she had struggled valiantly through changing tides that swept her first one way, then the other. It turned the 21-mile crossing into a gruelling, 65-mile slog.

    But yesterday – back in Blighty and recovering from her epic achievement – she declared: ‘Time and tide wait for no man – and they certainly didn’t wait for me.

    'I was fully expecting it to get dark before I got to Calais but I never imagined I’d also see the dawn again. But I wasn’t going to give up.’

    The previous record for the slowest crossing, set by Henry Sullivan at 26 hours and 50 minutes, had stood for 87 years before Mrs Cobell dipped her toe in the water at Dover last Saturday morning.

    There to greet her when she got to Calais late on Sunday morning (when they eventually found out where she would come ashore) were a welcome party bearing victory balloons and celebratory Champagne.

    But after glugging energy drinks throughout the previous day and night, Mrs Cobell eschewed the bubbly in favour of a nice warm towel.

    Her remarkable feat raised more than £2,000 in charity sponsorship for research into Huntingdon’s disease, a sum that was continuing to grow yesterday as news of her achievement spread. That was why she did it. Quite how was another matter entirely.

    ‘I don’t really know myself,’ she said yesterday. ‘I just kept thinking of all the people I’d be letting down if I stopped.’

    Mrs Cobell, a council foster carer from Tonbridge, Kent, was nicknamed Flipper at school because she took to the water so well. But after bringing up daughters Sarah, 32, and Victoria, 30, she started to gain weight and at one stage tipped the scales at 20 stone.

    Five years ago she took up swimming again and decided to prepare for the Channel challenge to lose weight. She became much fitter – but, despite intensive training, no slimmer. So she had a gastric bypass operation in November and shed eight stone in as many months.

    Then came the big swim.

    Jackie Cobell two years ago, when she was training for her swim in a bid to shift some of her 20 stone weight. She has since has a gastric band and shed eight stone

    ‘I practised on Windermere,’ she said. ‘It’s about half the distance of the Channel so I just doubled it, added some extra time, and worked out I could probably get to Calais in about 16 hours.’

    A 36ft support boat crewed by a pilot, her husband David, trainer, official observer and friend sailed alongside her.

    But soon after leaving Dover a strong tide dragged her south instead of east. Then it took her north... south again... northeast... south-west... and, eventually, subsided to allow her to carry on eastwards.

    At one stage she came perilously close to collision with a tanker. Then, after less than eight hours, her left arm packed up from an earlier injury and left her swimming for a time like a rowing boat with only one oar.

    Mrs Cobell kept going with the help of a powdered energy drink mixed with water, handed to her from the boat and swigged as she turned on her back and propelled herself with her legs.

    ‘It tasted absolutely revolting,’ she said. ‘At one point I was swimming parallel with the coast instead of towards it, which I knew wasn’t right.

    'Every time I caught a glimpse of shore I thought I’d nearly made it. Then the tide would sweep me in another direction. I sang to keep myself going – “Keep on Running” [A No 1 hit for the Spencer Davis Group in 1965] – but I changed the lyrics to Keep On Swimming.

    ‘When they told me I was a record breaker I thought they were just having a joke – until I realise it was the record for the slowest crossing.’

    Would she do it again? ‘I think I might need a rest first,’ she said. ‘My arms ache like mad and my ribs are black and blue from crashing against the waves all that time. But maybe next time I might be a bit quicker.’

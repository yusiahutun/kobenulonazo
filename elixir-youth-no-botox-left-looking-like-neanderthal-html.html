    No laughing matter: Sally Brampton's face has now returned to normal after Botox left her frozen with a frown

    Recently, I had bad Botox. I am edging towards my mid-50s and, like most middle-aged women, I can look a bit tired. I didn't want to look young, just refreshed, as if I'd had a good night's sleep.

    I knew there was a downside to having the injections; the permanently surprised eyebrows, the scarily impassive face, but I thought that was the result of too many top-ups; Hollywood's addiction to permayouth.

    As a professional beauty writer, I also knew Botox could go badly wrong, creating uneven eyebrows and lopsided frowns, but I thought that was budget Botox at a local beauty salon or foreheads needled at cut-price Botox parties where toxins are handed out as freely as wine.

    I also knew that, in the right hands, Botox works like magic, so I took myself off to one of the top dermatologists in the country, 'Mr Botox' himself, Dr Jean-Louis Sebagh.

    I interviewed him before having the treatment so didn't pay for it, but his normal rates for Botox start at Â£400. He says that to do good Botox 'you must be an artist with a needle'.

    According to his website, he treats some of the most famous faces in the world and claims he doesn't just make women look younger, he makes them look better.

    So it never occurred to me that I might look worse. Much, much worse.

    Afterwards, my eyebrows were pushed so far down I looked constantly angry with a deep-set frown between my eyes.

    It takes five days for Botox to set in and when it does, it sets like concrete. My face was stuck in a paralysis of hostility and there was nothing I could do to change my expression.

    I looked so terrible a friend asked if I'd hit my head. I was too embarrassed to tell her it was self-inflicted. I felt like a vain and foolish idiot.

    The headaches were terrible and the pressure on my eyebrows caused a build up of fluid that made my eyelids swell. I looked like a train wreck.

    The worst part was how badly it affected my mood. I didn't only look angry; I felt it, too.

    At the time, I was seeing a therapist for, irony of all ironies, depression.

    'I feel upset and angry all the time,' I complained. 'Hardly surprising,' she said. 'If we want to lift our spirits, one of the ways we can do that is to smile. The opposite is also true. If you screw up your face in an angry expression, you'll feel angry, too.'

    She's right. Study after scientific study shows that controlling our facial expression controls our mood. 

    Beware Botox: Sally has vowed never to have the treatment again after she suffered 'unfortunate' side effects (file photo)

    We all know a smile helps defuse a potentially toxic situation, but the problem was that I could not smile, or my mouth could, but the rest of my face stayed set in a furious scowl.

    I got into fights without even trying. 'No need to be so bad tempered,' people said.

    No use explaining that I wasn't bad tempered. It was just the way I looked.

    I refused to go to parties, convinced no amount of make-up could make me look better. In fact, it made me look worse.

    I had to hold up my eyebrows to apply eye shadow and couldn't put on mascara unless I wanted my lashes to stick to my brows. I found it hard to leave the house.

    Cosmetic surgeon Dr Jean-Louis Sebagh carried out Sally's treatment: He admitted results do not always meet individual's expectations

    'Cheer up, love,' builders called cheerfully. I went back to see Dr Sebagh, who told me I had suffered an 'unfortunate side-effect' and there was nothing he could do. It would wear off eventually.

    'No need to get so angry,' he said. I was close to tears. 'I'm not angry,' I said. 'You've made me look that way.'

    But in my view he was unapologetic - offering no regrets, just a Gallic shrug and a bottle of eye drops because my eyes were a little closed. The eye drops did nothing. I knew they wouldn't.

    I've had Botox before, just once, about five years ago, from another of the country's leading dermatologists, Dr Nick Lowe.

    It was so good I wrote an article for a newspaper saying: 'Now we've got Botox, who needs expensive face creams?'

    So it never occurred to me that, in expert hands, Botox could go so badly wrong. I knew the 'unfortunate' side-effect Dr Sebagh described is very rare (about one in 1,000).

    It is said to happen when a little Botox slips down from the injection site into the eyelid muscle to cause it to droop so alarmingly the eye almost closes. That's where the eye drops help.

    Eyelid droop usually only happens in the hands of a bad or inexperienced practitioner.

    It wore off and five months later, I am back to normal. It's such a relief to have my smile back that I will never have Botox again, no matter how tired I look.

    • Last night Dr Sebagh said: 'The vast majority of people who have Botox are pleased with the results and return for further treatment. On occasion, results sometimes do not meet the individual's expectations.'
